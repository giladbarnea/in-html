§1 · ConstitutionThe principles now steering the price

These emerged while you were answering, not as answers. They sit above every thread below and the whole proposal has to stay consistent with them. Hover a card and Lock it to ratify, or Shift-click to push back.

P1

Only estimate and commit to what you control.

Third-party work appears in the plan as a named block with no time label — Ran holds the whip, because he pays them and owns the relationship. I’ll make every call needed to drive it, but I never put a clock on someone else’s desk and then defend it. Your father-in-law’s rule. Retired the clock-stop clauses and every third-party time estimate.

P2

Price background automation by its value to Ran, not by my hours.

Unattended pipeline work isn’t priced as “hours it would take me without AI” — it’s priced against the money it saves and the opportunity it unlocks. Retired the itemized 38-hour pipeline table; pricing is more nuanced than a rate × time.

P3

Split the work by capability tier, measured by evals — not by time-effort.

Milestones step along an ability axis, from tier-0 (basic NL→SQL over the data) to tier-N (an experienced analyst running what-if and cause-and-effect work — categorically beyond queries). Each tier jump costs me a hands-on light-eval loop distilling wisdom into the harness, so each tier is a real unit of price. This also replaces “doesn’t suck” as the acceptance bar: a tier is accepted when it passes its eval set (§2).

§3 · SettledWhere we’ve landed

Definition of Done

Ran browses to a URL, logs in, and sees a ChatGPT-like chat — past sessions in a left sidebar — and has a back-and-forth in Hebrew with an autonomous agent (a Claude-Code/Codex-style process: a loop in the filesystem with tools, bash, Python, and read-only DuckDB access) that explores and iterates on ad-hoc analysis. The agent thinks and tool-uses in English; only the user-facing messages are Hebrew. Rudimentary, “no features.”

The old soft bar — “just doesn’t suck” — is dropped; acceptance is now a tier passing its eval set (§2).

settled

No refunds; pay per agile milestone. The DoD ships as several small milestones, each accepted and paid on its own. After every release, a feedback-planning-pricing (FP&P) session scopes and prices the next — the split itself is still §2’s job.
